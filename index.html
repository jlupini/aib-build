Less than 1% of eligible voters donate to federal political parties

And only a handful of them donate big

Just 0.05% of eligible voters donate more than $1000 to parties, yet this group accounts for almost a third of the total donations

So what can we do to change it?

We've got two exciting ideas.

First Big Idea:
We reduce the donation limit to $100

Right now, parties only connect with a handful of donors to fundraise, relying on a small number of people to get most of the way there.

This isn't a radical idea â€” it's been the case in Quebec for a long time, and even The Globe and Mail has called for a country-wide $100 limit.

2021 Globe and Mail editorial calling for a country-wide $100 limit

A $100 limit forces parties to connect with â€” and listen to â€” many more voters to earn donations

Lower the cap to what everyday people can afford, and we eliminate the outsized influence of superdonors in political finance. Right now, the median donation is just $60-90/year.

What about the tax credit?

With a $100 donation limit, we can eliminate the tax credit for political donations, freeing up all that public money for the second big idea...

Second Big Idea:
We bring back the per-vote-subsidy

With the tax credit eliminated from political donations, public funds should be distributed based on voter support at the ballot box.

This ensures that the public funding is aligned with the public voting

Under a per-vote subsidy, every voter becomes a donor, which means:

vote distribution = taxpayer money distribution

So not only does your vote count in choosing your MP, but it also counts towards how the party you supported fundraises

Parties are rewarded with more funding for every vote they earn.

How might political finance reform change the way you think about political donations and voting?

If parties had to fundraise by engaging a high volume of small donors and increasing voter turnout, they might be more responsive to the needs of Canadians.
